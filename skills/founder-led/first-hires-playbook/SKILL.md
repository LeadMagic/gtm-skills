# First Hires Playbook

## Overview

Your first 10 hires define your company's trajectory more than any product
decision. The mistake: hiring generalists with "good vibes" instead of
role-specific operators who can build the function from zero. This skill
covers when to hire each critical first role, how to write job descriptions
that attract builders (not corporate ladder-climbers), interview design,
compensation, and offer construction.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Role Definition

**Every first hire solves a specific business constraint, not a general "we need
help" feeling.** Define the constraint first:

- "I'm the bottleneck on product velocity" → Founding Engineer
- "I can't do enough discovery calls" → First SDR
- "I'm closing deals but churn is killing NRR" → First CSM
- "No one knows we exist" → First Marketer
- "I'm making decisions without data" → First Analyst

**Job description architecture for first hires:**
1. **Why this role exists:** "We're at $XM ARR, growing X% MoM. The constraint
   preventing us from reaching $YM is [specific constraint]. You'll own
   solving that."
2. **What you'll do:** 5-7 bullet points. Each starts with a verb. Each is
   measurable. No "help with" — only "own," "build," "design," "hire."
3. **Who you are:** Traits, not years of experience. "You've built X from
   scratch" > "7+ years of experience."
4. **Why join now:** Specific to this stage. "You'll be hire #8, shape the
   GTM function, and grow into VP as we scale."

**Anti-patterns to avoid in JDs:**
- "Rockstar/Ninja/Wizard" (cringe, filters out serious candidates)
- "7+ years of experience" (irrelevant for early stage — what did you BUILD?)
- "Nice to have: MBA" (filters out operators)
- Generic company description (candidates judge your writing — show taste)

### Phase 2: Sourcing

**Where first hires come from (in order of effectiveness):**
1. **Your network** (warm intros — highest signal)
2. **Investor network** (VC talent partners, portfolio company alumni)
3. **Targeted outbound** (find people doing the exact role at companies
   1-2 stages ahead of you)
4. **Communities** ( Pavilion, RevGenius, Lenny's community,
   specific Slack groups for the function)
5. **Job boards** (Work at a Startup, Wellfound, YC Jobs — avoid Indeed/LinkedIn
   spray unless you love filtering 500 resumes)

**Outbound sourcing template:**
```
Subject: [Mutual connection] said you're the best [role] in SaaS

Hi [name],

[Mutual connection] mentioned you when I asked "who's the best [role] you've
worked with?"

We're [company] — $X ARR, X% MoM growth, [one-line value prop]. We're hiring
our first [role] to [specific mission].

No obligation — would you be open to a 15-minute call to see if this is
interesting? Happy to share details but no pressure either way.

Best,
[Your name]
```

### Phase 3: Interview Process

**5-stage process for first hires (2-week clock):**

1. **Screen (30 min):** "Why are you interested? What have you built? What's
   your superpower?" Also: check for stage-fit. Someone who thrived at Google
   may drown at a 10-person startup.

2. **Work sample / Case study (async):** Give them a REAL problem you're
   facing. For a marketer: "Here's our ICP. Write the first 3 emails of a
   nurture sequence." For an engineer: "Here's a bug report. How would you
   debug it?" Real work > hypotheticals.

3. **Deep dive (60 min):** Walk through their work sample. Ask: "Walk me
   through your thinking." "What would you do differently with more time?"
   "What assumptions did you make?" This reveals HOW they think, not just
   what they produced.

4. **Team / Culture (30 min):** 2-3 team members. Assess: "Do I want to work
   with this person at 10pm on a Wednesday?" Technical skill is table stakes.
   Low-ego, high-ownership, direct communicators win at early stage.

5. **Founder close (30 min):** Sell the vision. Answer their hard questions.
   Ask: "What would make you say no?" Address concerns directly. Give them a
   decision timeline: "We'll have an answer by Friday."

**Red flags across all stages:**
- Blames previous employer/team for failures
- Can't articulate what they specifically built vs what their team built
- "I managed X" — you need builders, not managers (for first hires)
- No questions about the business (not curious = not invested)
- Takes more than 24 hours to return the work sample

### Phase 4: Compensation

**First 10 hire compensation framework:**

| Role | Salary Range | Equity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Founding Engineer | $140-200K | 1-3% | Technical co-founder territory at low end |
| First AE | $80-120K base / $160-240K OTE | 0.2-0.5% | 50/50 split |
| First SDR | $50-70K base / $80-110K OTE | 0.1-0.3% | Variable-heavy |
| First CSM | $80-120K | 0.2-0.5% | + variable for expansion |
| First Marketer | $100-150K | 0.3-0.8% | Head of/VP trajectory |
| First PM | $120-170K | 0.3-0.8% | Often promoted from within |
| Head of Eng | $170-220K | 0.5-1.5% | Critical — don't cheap out |

**Equity rules of thumb for first 10 hires:**
- 4-year vest, 1-year cliff, monthly thereafter
- Early exercise options (83b election — let them buy equity before it's
  valuable)
- Extended exercise window (10 years post-departure, not 90 days — this is
  standard for progressive startups)
- Be transparent: tell them the company's 409A valuation, total shares,
  and what their ownership % means

### Phase 5: Offer & Close

**Offer letter essentials:**
- Role, start date, reporting structure
- Salary, equity (number of shares + % of company), any variable comp
- Benefits summary
- At-will employment statement
- Confidentiality and IP assignment (attached separately)

**Closing first hires:**
- Speed matters. Top candidates get 3+ offers. Move from final interview to
  offer in under 48 hours.
- Give a deadline: "Offer is open until Friday at 5pm." Avoid exploding offers
  (less than 72 hours) unless there's genuine competitive pressure.
- Sell the stage: "At your last company you were hire #200. Here you'll be
  hire #8 and own the entire function. In 2 years you'll be VP."
- Involve your investors: Have lead investor call the candidate. It signals
  seriousness and gives them a relationship.

### Onboarding: First 90 Days

**Week 1: Context**
- "Here's everything: metrics, customers, problems, constraints."
- Meet every team member 1:1
- Read every customer call recording from last 30 days
- Shadow you for 2 days

**Week 1-2: First win**
- Give them a project they can ship in week 1-2. Something visible. Build
  confidence and credibility.

**Week 3-4: Ownership**
- Define their metrics: "You own X. We'll review weekly."
- Give them budget authority within their domain
- Let them make mistakes — don't micromanage

**Month 2-3: Scaling**
- They should be operating independently by day 60
- Weekly 1:1s (30 min) with CEO
- They start documenting their process (for their future hires)

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Hiring ahead of the pain.** "We'll need a marketing team eventually" is
   not a reason to hire a marketer at $500K ARR. The role will be undefined,
   they'll flounder, you'll fire them, it'll cost $50K+ in severance and
   recruiter fees. Fix: Hire when the constraint is active and painful, not
   theoretical.

2. **Hiring for pedigree over stage-fit.** The ex-Google PM who's never
   talked to a customer will fail at a 10-person startup. Fix: Prioritize
   "built it from zero" over "managed a $100M product line."

3. **Under-equitying first hires.** If you give first hires 0.1% and expect
   founder-level ownership mentality, you'll get employee-level effort. Fix:
   First 5 hires: 0.5-3%. First 10: 0.2-1.5%. These numbers scale down fast
   — hire #50 gets 0.05%.

4. **Slow offer process.** Top candidates are off the market in 2 weeks. If
   your process takes 4+ weeks, you'll only hire people who can't get offers
   elsewhere. Fix: 5 stages, 10 business days, offer in 48 hours.

5. **Skipping the work sample.** Interviews measure interview skills. Work
   samples measure work skills. The correlation between them is weaker than
   you think. Fix: Every role gets a real work sample before the final round.
