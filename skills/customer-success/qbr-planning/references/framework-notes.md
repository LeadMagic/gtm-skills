# Qbr Planning — Framework Notes

Use these references to ground outputs in named, repeatable methodology.

## Primary Frameworks

- **Gainsight — QBR Methodology.** Gainsight's QBR framework structures the review around value realization, not product roadmaps. Three pillars: (1) Value Delivered — quantified ROI since last QBR using agreed-upon success metrics, (2) Value at Risk — adoption gaps, unused features, support trends that threaten renewal, (3) Value to Capture — expansion opportunities, new use cases, cross-sell motions aligned to the customer's stated Desired Outcomes.

- **Winning by Design — Bowtie Metrics.** The Bowtie model's post-sale metrics (NRR, GRR, expansion pipeline, time-to-value, support SLA adherence) form the data backbone of QBRs. Every QBR slide should map to a Bowtie metric. The Bowtie's expansion stage is the QBR's commercial purpose: QBRs that don't produce an expansion or advocacy action are status updates, not business reviews.

- **Gainsight — Customer Success Maturity Model.** QBR design varies by CS maturity: Level 1 (reactive) QBRs are product usage reviews; Level 2 (managed) QBRs are value reviews with ROI quantification; Level 3 (optimized) QBRs are strategic planning sessions where the customer's QBR feeds your product roadmap. The QBR template should match the maturity level.

## Operating Assumptions

- QBRs are for executive alignment, not product training. The audience is the Economic Buyer and Champion — present business outcomes, not feature demos.
- Every QBR must end with a mutual action plan: customer commits to X (adoption step, reference, expansion), vendor commits to Y (feature, support escalation, roadmap visibility).
- QBR cadence: quarterly for enterprise ($50K+ ACV), semi-annual for mid-market ($5K-$50K ACV), annual or automated health check for SMB.
