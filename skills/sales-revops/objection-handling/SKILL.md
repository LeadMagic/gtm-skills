# Objection Handling

## Overview

Objections aren't rejections — they're information. A prospect who says
"we don't have budget" might mean "I don't see enough value to create
budget." The difference determines whether you walk away or win the deal.

This skill builds a structured objection handling system: a 6-category
taxonomy, the AER framework (Acknowledge → Explore → Redirect), and
pre-handling objections in outreach sequences before they're raised.

## When to Use

- "Build an objection handling playbook"
- "How do I respond when a prospect says [objection]?"
- "Create objection battlecards for our sales team"
- "Prepare responses for common pushback"
- "Handle the 'too expensive' objection"
- "What do I say when they mention [competitor]?"

## Authoritative Foundations

Force Management's Command of the Message framework teaches that objections
reveal gaps in the Value Messaging Framework — if a prospect doesn't see
the value, the Before Scenario wasn't compelling or the PBOs weren't tied
to their specific pain.

Sandler's Pain Funnel surfaces the real objection: budget objections are
usually value objections in disguise, timing objections are usually priority
objections. Never answer the first objection — explore what's behind it.

Challenger's reframing principle: when a prospect objects from a mistaken
assumption, teach them something that changes how they see the problem.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Build Objection Taxonomy

Categorize every objection your team hears:

| Category | Examples | Root Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Value / ROI** | "Too expensive," "No budget" | Haven't quantified the pain or proven the ROI |
| **Timing** | "Not right now," "Next quarter" | Pain isn't urgent enough to prioritize |
| **Competition** | "We use [competitor]," "They're cheaper" | Haven't differentiated clearly |
| **Authority** | "I need to check with [boss]" | Haven't engaged the economic buyer |
| **Status Quo** | "We're fine with current solution" | Haven't made the cost of inaction real |
| **Trust / Risk** | "Too risky," "Not sure it'll work" | Insufficient social proof or technical validation |

### Phase 2: AER Framework

For every objection, structure the response:

1. **Acknowledge** — Validate the concern. "That makes total sense."
   Never argue. Acknowledgment builds trust.

2. **Explore** — Ask a question that uncovers what's behind the objection.
   "When you say budget is tight — is it a priority question or a
   timing question?" Sandler Pain Funnel: go deeper before answering.

3. **Redirect** — Reframe with insight, proof, or a new angle.
   "We actually see that companies who invest now during [condition]
   capture [outcome], because [insight]. Here's what [similar company] saw."

### Phase 3: Pre-Handling in Outreach

The best objection response is one you never need to give. Pre-handle
common objections in your outreach sequence:

| Objection | Pre-Handle in Sequence |
|---|---|
| Timing | "Most teams tell us they wish they'd started 3 months sooner — here's why" |
| Budget | "Companies our size typically see [ROI figure] within [timeframe]" |
| Competition | "Unlike [competitor], we [differentiator] — here's what that meant for [customer]" |

### Phase 4: Objection Battlecards

Create per-competitor battlecards with objection-specific responses:

```
Objection: "[Competitor] is cheaper"
Acknowledge: "They are less expensive — I see why you'd compare on price."
Explore: "When you look at the total cost including [factor], does that
change the math?"
Redirect: "Customers who switched from [competitor] to us actually saved
[amount] because [reason]. Here's the breakdown."
Proof: [Case study with specific ROI data]
```

## Output Format

Objection handling playbook with taxonomy, AER responses per objection
(20-30+ objections), pre-handling sequence inserts, and per-competitor
objection battlecards.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Answering before exploring.** "Too expensive" → "But we're worth it!"
   The first objection is rarely the real objection. Explore first.

2. **Arguing with the prospect.** "That's not true, actually..." You may
   win the argument and lose the deal. Acknowledge, then redirect.

3. **Generic responses.** "We have great ROI" isn't an objection response.
   "Customers in your industry see 3x ROI within 6 months. Here's how
   [similar company] did it." — that's a response.

4. **No competitive objection prep.** If you know the competitor's cheaper,
   prepare the response before it comes up. Pre-handling beats reacting.

5. **One-size responses.** A CTO's security objection needs a different
   response than a VP Sales' budget objection. Tailor by persona.
